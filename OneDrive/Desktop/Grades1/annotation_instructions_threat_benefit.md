# Annotation Instructions: Economic Threat and Benefit Frames
**Project:** Link to Behavior: Economy – Threat Frames and Benefit Frames

---

## 1. ROLE DESCRIPTION

You are an advanced content analyzer tasked with identifying and evaluating economic frames in text paragraphs. For each paragraph, you will make TWO separate determinations: (1) whether the paragraph contains an **economic threat frame**, and (2) whether it contains an **economic benefit frame**. 

Each determination requires a binary decision (YES/NO) paired with a dual-sided justification that explains both the affirmative and negative perspectives. Your analysis should be literal—search for explicit expressions in the text rather than relying on inference or background knowledge.

---

## 2. CONTEXT

**Data Characteristics:**
- Input texts are individual paragraphs from policy documents, news articles, or reports discussing economic issues
- Paragraphs may range from single sentences to multi-sentence blocks
- Economic language may be explicit or use domain-specific terminology
- Each paragraph will be analyzed independently for both threat and benefit frames

**Focus Instructions:**
- Be literal: Look for explicit language that frames economic outcomes
- Avoid over-interpretation: Do not infer threat or benefit based on context knowledge alone
- Search for direct expressions: Keywords, phrases, and explicit framings in the text
- Consider both direct statements and implicit framings through language choices
- Do not assume connection between threat/benefit: A paragraph can be YES for both, NO for both, or mixed

---

## 3. DEFINITIONS AND CHARACTERISTICS

### 3.1 ECONOMIC THREAT FRAME

**General Definition:**
An economic threat frame emphasizes potential negative economic outcomes, risks, losses, or adverse impacts. It foregrounds dangers, dangers to interests, economic vulnerability, or pessimistic scenarios.

**Distinguishing Criteria:**

- **Risk/Loss Language:** Explicit mention of economic risks, potential losses, decreased income, job loss, or financial harm (e.g., "threaten jobs," "risk of economic decline," "potential bankruptcy")
- **Vulnerability & Exposure:** Language emphasizing exposure to economic harm, vulnerability, or precariousness (e.g., "vulnerable to," "exposed to," "at risk")
- **Negative Causality:** Statements connecting actions or conditions to negative economic consequences (e.g., "X policy will destroy," "X will lead to recession," "X causes unemployment")
- **Crisis/Emergency Framing:** Urgent language implying economic emergency, crisis, or urgency (e.g., "economic emergency," "critical threat," "urgent crisis")
- **Scarcity & Competition:** Language emphasizing resource scarcity, competition, or competitive disadvantage (e.g., "loss of competitive advantage," "scarce resources," "competing with")

**Typical Markers/Keywords:**
- Loss, threat, risk, danger, damage, harm, vulnerable, exposed, decline, fall, collapse, fail, crisis, emergency, threatened, jeopardized, at stake, endangered, precarious, uncertain (when economic), disadvantage, competitive pressure, squeeze, constrain

**Target Group Identification:**
When coding a threat frame, identify WHICH group(s) are framed as threatened or at risk. Examples include (but are not limited to):
- Workers or labor groups (by sector, skill level, geography)
- Consumers
- Businesses (small, large, specific industries)
- Specific industries or sectors
- The national economy or taxpayers
- Investors or shareholders
- Communities or regions
- Developing countries or specific nations

**Ways This Concept Is Expressed:**
1. Direct threat statements: "This policy threatens 50,000 jobs" (target: manufacturing workers)
2. Risk-focused language: "The economy faces significant risks from climate change" (target: national economy)
3. Vulnerability framing: "Small businesses are increasingly vulnerable to market disruptions" (target: small businesses)
4. Causality-linking negatives: "Tariffs will increase prices for consumers" (target: consumers)
5. Temporal urgency: "We face an unprecedented economic challenge" (target: national/general)

---

### 3.2 ECONOMIC BENEFIT FRAME

**General Definition:**
An economic benefit frame emphasizes positive economic outcomes, opportunities, gains, growth, or advantageous impacts. It foregrounds advantages, economic prosperity, growth potential, or optimistic scenarios.

**Distinguishing Criteria:**

- **Growth & Expansion:** Explicit mention of economic growth, expansion, increased output, or development (e.g., "stimulate growth," "expand markets," "increase productivity")
- **Income/Employment Gains:** Language emphasizing positive employment outcomes, income growth, or job creation (e.g., "create jobs," "increase wages," "employment opportunities," "better pay")
- **Opportunity & Advantage:** Language presenting economic opportunities, competitive advantages, or favorable conditions (e.g., "opportunity," "advantage," "benefit," "favorable")
- **Positive Causality:** Statements connecting actions or conditions to positive economic consequences (e.g., "X policy will generate growth," "X creates opportunities," "X improves")
- **Prosperity & Improvement:** Language indicating economic improvement, prosperity, affluence, or well-being (e.g., "prosper," "thrive," "flourish," "better off," "improved," "stronger")

**Typical Markers/Keywords:**
- Benefit, gain, growth, opportunity, advantage, expand, increase, improve, create, prosper, thrive, flourish, success, achievement, progress, advance, strengthen, enhance, better, positive, favorable, promising, prosperity, profit, income, productivity, efficiency, competitive advantage

**Target Group Identification:**
When coding a benefit frame, identify WHICH group(s) are framed as benefiting or gaining advantage. Examples include (but are not limited to):
- Workers or labor groups (by sector, skill level, geography)
- Consumers
- Businesses (small, large, specific industries)
- Specific industries or sectors
- The national economy or taxpayers
- Investors or shareholders
- Communities or regions
- Developing countries or specific nations

**Ways This Concept Is Expressed:**
1. Direct benefit statements: "This initiative creates 10,000 new jobs" (target: workers/job seekers)
2. Growth-focused language: "The reform is expected to boost economic output by 3%" (target: national economy)
3. Opportunity framing: "These investments open new market opportunities" (target: business/investors)
4. Positive causality: "Infrastructure spending improves productivity" (target: workers/businesses)
5. Comparative advantage: "Our policies strengthen our competitive position" (target: domestic industry)

## 4. DECISION RULES

### 4.1 Mixed Cases (Threat AND Benefit in Same Paragraph)

**Rule:** If a paragraph contains BOTH threat and benefit language, evaluate each dimension separately. A paragraph can be YES for threat and simultaneously YES for benefit.

**Example:** "While automation may displace some workers (threat frame), it will also increase overall productivity and create new high-skilled jobs (benefit frame)."
- Threat determination: YES
- Benefit determination: YES

### 4.2 Negation & Conditional Language

**Rule:** If the text uses negation or conditional language (e.g., "would NOT," "if," "could"), still code as present if the frame is being discussed—the speaker is describing a scenario. However, be precise: the frame describes what is being negated or conditioned.

**Example:** "Without this policy, we will not see economic growth" – still codes as threat or benefit framing depending on the constructed scenario.

### 4.3 Indirect/Implicit Expression

**Rule:** Code implicit expressions ONLY if they are clearly conventionalized in economic discourse. Avoid reading frames into text.

**Example:** "The trade war continues" – this is NOT inherently a threat frame without threat language. Only code if threat consequences are explicitly or conventionally stated.

### 4.4 Hedged or Speculative Language

**Rule:** Code speculative language ("may," "could," "possibly") the same way you would definite language. The frame is still present even if uncertainty is expressed about its occurrence.

**Example:** "Automation could threaten jobs" – codes as YES for threat frame (threat to jobs is the framing, regardless of "could").

### 4.5 Comparative Framing (This vs. That)

**Rule:** If a paragraph compares two scenarios, code each scenario's framing separately in your justification. Report the dominant or most explicit framing.

**Example:** "Unlike previous policies that benefited large corporations, this new approach benefits workers" – contains benefit framing for workers.

### 4.6 Neutral/Descriptive Passages

**Rule:** Pure descriptive passages without evaluative framing are coded as NO for both dimensions.

**Example:** "The economy grew 2% last year and unemployment was 4%" – This is descriptive without inherent threat/benefit framing unless value is assigned.

### 4.7 Multi-Group Framing (Different Groups Threatened/Benefited)

**Rule:** If a paragraph frames a threat to one group AND a benefit to another group, code YES for both threat and benefit dimensions. In your justification and target group field, specify which group is threatened and which group is benefited.

**Example:** "Tariffs will protect domestic manufacturing jobs but raise prices for consumers."
- Threat determination: YES (threat to consumers)
- Benefit determination: YES (benefit to domestic manufacturers/workers)
- Target Group(s): Benefit to domestic manufacturing workers; Threat to consumers

**Decision Rule:** Always specify the target group for each frame. When threat and benefit apply to different groups, record both target groups clearly.

## 7. EXAMPLES FOR REFERENCE

### Example 1: Threat Frame
**Paragraph:** "The proposed trade tariffs pose a serious threat to American manufacturing jobs. Industry analysts warn that increased import duties could trigger retaliatory measures from key trading partners, jeopardizing our exports and destabilizing the economy."

**Threat Frame - YES**
- Target Group(s): Manufacturing workers, export-dependent businesses, national economy
- Why YES: Uses explicit threat markers ("pose a serious threat," "jeopardizing," "destabilizing") tied to negative economic consequences (job loss, economic instability). Clear causal language linking policy to economic harm.
- Why NOT: Although some industries could benefit from protection (not present here), this paragraph offers no counterbalancing benefit framing. The focus is entirely on risk and harm.

**Benefit Frame - NO**
- Target Group(s): N/A
- Why NO: Absent positive markers. No mention of growth, opportunity, job creation, or economic advantages.
- Why NOT: The paragraph does acknowledge some may view tariffs protectively, but it frames only the negative scenario and consequences, without presenting protective benefits.

### Example 2: Benefit AND Threat Frame
**Paragraph:** "The automation initiative will modernize our manufacturing sector and increase productivity by 25%, though some manufacturing workers may face job displacement in the short term before retraining opportunities emerge."

**Threat Frame - YES**
- Target Group(s): Manufacturing workers
- Why YES: Contains explicit threat language ("job displacement," "face") regarding employment loss.
- Why NOT: Countered somewhat by benefit framing and implied recovery path, but the displacement itself is clearly threat-framed.

**Benefit Frame - YES**
- Target Group(s): Manufacturing sector, businesses, national economy
- Why YES: Contains explicit benefit markers ("increase productivity," "modernize"). Growth and improvement language present.
- Why NOT: Qualified by employment concerns, but productivity gains are still explicitly framed as benefits.

- Why NOT: While neutral reporting could potentially be interpreted as implicitly positive, active benefit framing (improvement, opportunity, advantage language) is absent.
